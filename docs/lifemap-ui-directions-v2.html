Four bets on a calmer mind.

LifeMap exists to lighten the mental load of family-admin for parents and ADHD brains. So these four directions all render the exact same moment — your next three things — and each one is a different wager on how to make that moment land. Every choice traces to a researched principle, not a mood. The reasoning lives in lifemap-design-rationale.md.

The six principles

Drawn from cognitive load theory, ADHD/executive-function research, calm technology, and WCAG cognitive-accessibility guidance. Each direction below leans on a different subset.

P1
One clear next action
Reduce to a single doable step; fewer options = faster, less stuck.
P2
Minimize choices & chunk
Working memory is tiny (~3–4 live chunks). Group, cap, default.
P3
Low visual noise
Clutter is extraneous load. Whitespace; color only where it counts.
P4
Forgiving tone
Reversible, non-judgmental, safe to leave and come back to.
P5
Show now, hide later
Progressive disclosure; the backlog doesn't trigger overwhelm.
P6
Externalize memory
The app holds state & timing so the brain doesn't have to.

Why it works for the ADHD / parent brain

Leans hardest on calm and tone.

  • P6The "Due next / Waiting on" footer makes timing and external blockers visible, so they don't have to be held in memory.

02 Focus-first one thing at a time

The bet: the backlog is the enemy. Show one action, huge and alone, on a dark stage. The other two are a dim whisper at the bottom you can ignore. Betting that the fastest way to start is to remove everything you're not doing right now.

Why it works for the ADHD / parent brain

Leans hardest on a single action and disclosure.

P1P2 P3P4 P5P6
  • P1The screen answers exactly one question — "what do I do right now?" — with one concrete, pre-decided step. Nothing to triage, so there's nothing to stall on.
  • P5The other two tasks are literally pushed out of focus (dim, bottom, smaller). The mountain is hidden, so it can't trigger the overwhelm that stops people cold.
  • P2One choice, one button. Decision time collapses when the option count drops to one recommended action.
EMPHASIS
03 Low-stimulus sensory-load floor

The bet: do less to the eye. Near-monochrome, no gradients, no shadows, almost no color — one tiny sage dot is the loudest thing on screen. Maximal whitespace. Betting that for a sensory-overloaded brain, the kindest design is the quietest one.

Why it works for the ADHD / parent brain

Leans hardest on noise reduction and chunking.

P1P2 P3P4 P5P6
  • P3Every removed flourish gives back processing power. No gradients, no cards, no shadow — extraneous load drops to near zero, which calm-tech research treats as the goal, not a compromise.
  • P2The list is three items, period, with the time-sensitive one weighted heavier. Working memory isn't asked to hold a queue.
  • P1The single sage dot is the only saturated pixel — it silently marks the one thing that matters today, doing the focal work that color usually shouts for.
EMPHASIS
04 Scaffolded externalize working memory

The bet: be the external brain. Explicit chunks, real checkboxes, a progress bar, and status on everything. It looks more "tool" than the others on purpose — betting that visible structure and crossable boxes are what actually hold state for a brain that won't.

Why it works for the ADHD / parent brain

Leans hardest on chunking and externalized memory.

P1P2 P3P4 P5P6
  • P6The explicit copy — "I'm holding everything below" — plus persistent checkboxes and a "handled" chunk make the app a trusted external system, the single most effective executive-function support.
  • P2Items are grouped into labeled chunks (time-sensitive / this week / handled) with counts, so a flat overwhelming list becomes three small, scannable groups.
  • P4The progress bar and crossed-off row give visible credit for what's done — momentum and reassurance instead of an ever-growing pile.

So — which bet fits your user?

They're not just four looks; they're four theories of what an overwhelmed parent needs most. Haven bets on warmth, Focus-first on subtraction, Low-stimulus on quiet, Scaffolded on structure. Pick one (or a combination) and it becomes the language for Today, Calendar, Capture, and the rest.
